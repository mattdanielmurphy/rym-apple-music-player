Fantasy (2025)
DJ Sabrina the Teenage DJ
Review by oursinkingship
it's not that you can't release tens of hours of material over the course of a 15-year stretch across several albums containing essential components of your music only to then drop your second-best LP in your already massive discography, it's just nobody can do that so why would DJ Sabrina be able to?

i have had a personal rule to wait until the middle of December at the earliest to begin finalizing my end-of-the-year list. December is still part of the year and i would hate to not include something truly deserving. in 2025, i can't think of better vindication than the dancefloor rhythms provided by "Fantasy," DJ Sabrina's second four-hour studio album. if you're unfamiliar with DJ Sabrina, she has a reputation for weaving together full sets that function as an album front to back. tracks will seamlessly transition into each other or enter interludes that bridge together the previous and upcoming song only to take off on its own once finding its melody. "Fantasy," far being from the exception, might become yet another shining example of this feat.

the first ten tracks of this album could've been released as a standalone and would've been considered one of the best DJ Sabrina albums; required listening for those interested in starting somewhere digestible. then, somehow, we are gifted 30 additional tracks that justify the remainder of the album. before i realized it, the four hours were over and i was going over all the new choruses and chord progressions that were quickly becoming all-time favorites, such as the hypnotically engaging "All These Years" and "Hideaway."

if you are a fan of House, already a DJ Sabrina fan, or are looking for something completely new; perhaps a worthwhile, rewarding, and challenging listen, may i strongly recommend "Fantasy" as your next sonic journey.

Standout Tracks: "All These Years," "So Far Away," "Hideaway," "Will My Love," "Rainfalls"

Obstinacy: Sisyphean Dreams Unfolded (2025)
Fleshvessel
Review by pecoraroryan1992
WHAT A WEIRDO!!!
Nowadays, death metal can be super experimental. From ambient influences all the way to jazz, bands have been thinking a lot more outside-the-box than ever before. One of the leading groups in that experimental category is a five-piece band from Chicago, Fleshvessel. Their first album, Yearning: Promethean Fates Sealed was an absolutely wonky slab of brutal/avant-garde death metal full of jazz-fusion passages and progressive riffage that added a bit of a symphonic aspect and even some black metal bits. It really threw a wrench into what death metal is and can be. Their brand new album, Obstinacy: Sisyphean Dreams Unfolded continues in the same vein while kicking up all of the quirky influences up to ten.

These guys are experts in off-kilter riffage, whacky vocal performances and an overall atmosphere of being lost on a newly discovered planet surrounded by ugly creatures and no oxygen to breathe normally. This album suffocates you in the best way possible. Look no further than the opening track, ‘Mental Myiasis’, for some of the weirdest death metal you will ever possibly hear. These guys incorporate all kinds of different instruments apart from your normal guitars, bass and drums, incorporating triangles, chimes and even some Puerto Rican folk instruments as well as a lot of acoustics thrown into the mix. This isn't an ordinary death metal album and the guys make sure you know that right from the beginning.

‘Am’ is the second track here and it really brings a cinematic vibe to an already over-the-top album with cosmic atmospheres and symphonic arrangements that would put Dimmu Borgir to shame. The boys in Fleshvessel really know how to make an album sound like an actual motion picture event. The vocals are operatic, spastic and really show off the range of singer/lunatic Troll Hart perfectly. The riffs, courtesy of Sakda and Alexander, are absolutely mind-bending and the drumming from Colin is a clinic on rhythm applied to all of the chaos. ‘Cessation Fixation’ is a track that really shows off the bass work here, also courtesy of Sadka, who is an absolute madman with the throbbing basslines.

‘It Lurched from a Chasm in the Sky’ is the final track and is also the longest track here at nearly 17 minutes. This mammoth of a track is a cosmic, jazzy epic that almost reminds the listener of a death metal version of Tangerine Dream with spacey synths and King Diamond-esque vocals that really show off the pure atmospheric aspect of the band. This track is a culmination of the three previous tracks and almost reminds the listener of a mutated version of Blood Incantation. It really makes you feel as if you're floating in the solar system with no spacesuit and gasping for air while you slowly suffocate.

Obstinacy: Sisyphean Dreams Unfolded, simply put, is one hell of a weird ride, but in all the right ways. The musicianship is absolutely off the charts and the songwriting is to die for. Even with all of the jerky riffage and chaotic atmospheres, you can tell that these guys are masters of their instruments. It might sound like a complete mess, and it most definitely is, the band is still extremely tight in their playing. Every single bit is right where it's supposed to be and you can tell that the slop they're stringing together is intentional. This kind of death metal definitely isn't for everyone, but if you want experimental death metal that you can have fun with, look no further than Fleshvessel.

Flood (2000)
Boris
Review by UprfontMarlin65
Repetition is an odd thing. Often considered monotonous and lazy, it’s a very difficult concept to balance well, let alone create beauty through. At face value, repetition seems to offer comfort in the delivery of something we already know and enjoy, utilising familiarity to its advantage. While this is often the case, the best repetition thoughtfully explores the power of subtlety and gradual transition to transform an object into something else entirely. It’s a testament to the facilitation of time as a force of movement. That’s where Japanese drone-metal band Boris enter with their 2000 album Flood. Despite Boris’ roots as a predominantly metal-influenced group, at its core, Flood offers a broader exploration of genre than other typical releases of the band. Deconstructing Flood, which is built upon 4 distinct compositional passages, sees the movement through minimalism, post-rock, drone-metal and finally ambient. While tackling the 4 of these genres on a single album should be considered an impressive triumph in and of itself, where Boris succeed above all expectations is through their skill in tying each passage together into a formidable body of work.

Flood begins cautiously, introducing simple, pleasant guitar plucking with the faint background sounds of the drop of a cassette deck. It doesn’t take long before it becomes evident that there’s more at play. The guitar begins to lose its sense of harmony and fall out of sync ever so slowly, making minutes seem like seconds through its defining trance. With the introduction of compressed drumming bursts that are honestly best described as cannon fire, the uneasy tension builds to a crashing jaw-dropping crescendo. Flood II picks up immediately where the first track leaves off, now seeking to rebuild the sense of peace that was crumbled to dust throughout the opener. Easily the standout track, Flood II’s incessantly slow progression backed by the gentle ticking drums is exceptional. The explorative guitar melodies are gorgeous beyond words and the final extended, piercing notes could ring out forever and I’d be happy.

Flood III is where Boris reveal their true colours as a drone metal band. Calling back to the ending of Flood I, the song revels in destruction and noisy catharsis. Featuring the only vocal section on the record, the focus isn’t to make things faster but instead to accent everything from before until it’s sufficiently louder and fuzzier. It’s an incredibly dense and viscous affair, yet still fluid, shifting between drum patterns and guitar rhythms amongst the haze. Finally we are led into the closer where clarity is opened once more, refreshing breaths of air providing relief from the prior dense onslaught. Soft echoey sounds gently push out of the water, the journey finally completed as all movement comes to an eventual standstill in the form of a quiet fade out. The final minutes see the subtlest of drone notes stretch outwards, crawling forward purposelessly before evaporating into silence.

Flood isn’t merely the construction and compilation of these 4 predominantly genre-seperate passages, which instead merge between one another flawlessly through clever production decisions and compositional directions. The ebb and flow between the louder abrasive segments contrast excellently against the lighter drawn-out guitar work. In doing so, it allows Flood to merge into a wider narrative that progresses organically along its extended runtime. Due to the specific nature and the progression of the LP, I often draw comparisons between the music and typical fiction narrative. An introduction (Flood I), exploration and building of characters (Flood II), creation of tension into an eventual climax (Flood III) and prolonged resolution (Flood IV), are all present, exhibiting more than enough elements to construct an epic story. This creativity in the intertwining of music with narrative is breathtaking and further encourages listeners to embellish their own imagination in developing some awe-inspiring personal story that Flood represents to them. In my eyes, the tale is something comparable to the film Seven Samurai, another Japanese piece, which is one of the the purest expressions of the traditional narrative structure that I’ve come across. Plus, Seven Samurai features a lot of rain.

Flood is a lesson in patience. It strives to flow around you, washing and cleansing as its gorgeous melodies and droning notes swirl into a whirlpool, sucking and drowning out the rest of the world, its flood creeping slowly in. It’s undoubtedly an easy album to get lost in and one of the most important front-to-back listens in existence. Putting it on shuffle would simply not do it justice. Both gorgeous and meditative, while at times all-encompassing, Flood’s diverse textures are unrivalled and unmatched, solidifying the album’s fluid depth as a progressive, un-presumptuous masterwork.

Cabin in the Sky (2025)
De La Soul
Review by lilikoipassion
'Legend Has It' #6: Cabin in the Sky by De La Soul
way better than it has any right being.

de la soul are my favorite hip hop artists ever. even beyond their legendary run of the first four classics we know and love, i always found that their music and spirit remained insanely creative and positive for a sound so important and intertwined with the east coast scene of the nineties. when trugoy passed away almost three years ago, it broke my heart into pieces. there was now a total bittersweet mist in the air surrounding the massive year that 2023 was for the group: their entire back catalogue finally making it onto streaming, numerous represses and brand deals, tons of concerts, and a lengthy press run covering all their accomplishments for hip hop as well as their plans for the future.

a 'new' de la soul album has been in the talks for a long time now, even before they were getting close to obtaining the rights to their original music years back. during that press run i mentioned above, the teasing was mainly surrounding a new installment in the AOI series, and how it was gonna be produced and handled entirely by prince paul. given how naturally the plans of a lot of promised albums never really make it to fruition, i was never truly optimistic about us getting it soon. i just had to find some satisfaction in the really disjointed 'and the Anonymous Nobody,' i guess.

you're on the rym page for this album, you get the point, it exists now. if you've seen my past words on the currently active 'Legend Has It' campaign, you'll know how mismatched a lot of the label's output has felt these past few months. seeing de la's name amongst the others excited me at first, but after so many seemingly rushed or sloppy second records started to roll out, i got more and more worried. and when the singles for 'Cabin in the Sky' started to dispense, i was still clinging onto hope, even if this big, looming fear of quality was at the forefront of my mind. how could this album be good at all if they only started working on it february of this year? that's just kinda where my headspace was at before this record came out. sure, the credits looked nice, it had a solid and long length to it, but god, i've just been losing more and more hope for this stretch of releases over time.

which is why i'm so pleased that this album feels like a natural, modern take on the sound and style of de la soul that i absolutely adore. twenty songs leaves a lot to live up to, but considering the fact that only one of those is a skit, it's shocking to me that they were able to hit the mark on pretty much each and every track. the beats sound like a really modern & bright spin on classic boom bap (mostly thanks to veterans of the sound like pete rock & dj premier, as well as long time collaborators like supa dave west) and each feature tends to fit into the song like a glove.

they only decided to use a select few of dove's recordings on these songs, which thankfully makes sure the album doesn't pretend to be what it isn't. this isn't the AOI3 that they teased before (whether it's completely scrapped or planned for future tinkering, nobody knows). this is a record that was conceived just for this specific label campaign - it wasn't made or assembled with any input from dove before his passing at all, and it's very noble that they made the listener aware of that. with trugoy's absence, posdnuos is the main rapper here, and he's able to keep the life up very well. even almost forty years into his career, he's still such a charming emcee that you tend to only get earnest feelings from. he commands the spotlight super well by himself, and brings the exact type of conscious, humorous, and introspective bars you're expecting. and while his flow still feels a bit weary, it's certainly improved from the last couple of verses he's put out these past few years. i don't think anyone who loves de la soul would be going into an album of theirs (especially a brand new one) for technical skills, anyways.

and while the subject matter and nature of 'Cabin in the Sky' is often light, it has these tender and emotional sweet spots that make the album feel like it has its own sense of clarity. while the loss of dave is at the forefront of everyone's mind, there's a lot of songs like 'A Quick 16 for Mama,' 'Palm of His Hands,' and the title track that offer a different slice of coverage for family, religion, and death itself. the title of the album is a reference to death already - looking at the album cover and seeing two men carrying their third in a cloud-bound cabin in the sky like a coffin should be enough to explain the imagery they're trying to get at - but it's because of these little moments that the album overall feels like a celebration of not just dave's life, but life itself, as well as a serious reflection on the time we all have left on this planet. which i really love. it's a super poignant point to dwell on, but it makes this record feel like a monument in de la soul's already amazing discography. not only is it a return to form, but a wisely crafted testament to the importance of the art, connections, and life they've breathed into existence with their careers.

'Legend Has It' wasn't an excuse for pos & mase to scrape together what they could with past recordings, it gave them a reason to finally see another new album to completion. the album they wanted to make, inspired by all the bittersweet feelings that must've come up these past two years. and one of dove's silly, solo home demos being placed at the very end of the tracklist shows exactly who their #1 inspiration for this album was.

thank you dave <3

London Calling (1979)
The Clash
Review by MoeHartman
It's funny how The Clash are considered one of the quintessential punk bands when only their first album was truly punk, but even if they did leave their roots behind quickly, it was all for the better. London Calling is often called one of the all-time great albums, and the claim inspires no arguments from me.

Joe Strummer is one of the all-time great songwriters with lots of lyrical variety, tackling, among other subjects, politics. Nonetheless, he avoids a problem that his peers, such as Jon King of Gang of Four, tended to have. He never sounds preoccupied with this political content, singing about all sorts of things. Sometimes, it's even ambiguous, and he could be singing about almost anything.

On the musical side of things, sure, it doesn't have the raw intensity of their debut, but it doesn't need it. Instead, The Clash do Beatles-like genre hopping. Here and there instrumentation is punctuated with lots of horns and even some occasional keyboards, and that's another thing they had on their peers. They were willing to experiment. The album's variety of styles is hard to match, with some songs more in line with their more rock oriented sound of their first two, such as the brilliant title track, "Death or Glory," or "The Clampdown." There is also a clear reggae influence, and songs like, "Rudie Can't Fail," "Guns of Brixton," and "Revolution Rock" display this mostly clearly. There are even outright pop songs like "Lost in the Supermarket" or "Spanish Bombs."

The best thing about the album, though, is that no matter what the band are doing, it's amazing. There are a lot of songs here, and looking at the tracklisting, I think, "There must be some song that will let the album down." An example would be, "The Right Profile," but when the songs plays, I think, "Oh that's this song. I love this song."

London Calling one of those albums where the songs are so accessible, strong, and varied that it could be a greatest hits album. Even though the album is almost 20 songs it never drags or overstays its welcome. It's a remarkable feat, and one that they sadly would not fully repeat on the even longer Sandinista. I've held off on giving this one a perfect score due to its reputation. With so many songs it's hard to judge whether it's truly perfect, but whenever I listen to it, I think, "This could be one of the best albums I've heard." That's good enough for me. Why leave it at "could be", when it clearly "is"?

Blizzard (2025)
Dove Ellis
Review by Mozip
Every year, the first end-of-year lists start appearing in early December. Whether it’s because publications want to be ahead of the competition or because they want to start the new year with a clean slate, it remains a fact. Unfortunately, this means that some albums always end up missing from these lists. Last year, that was the fantastic Heavy Metal by Cameron Winter, the singer of Geese. An emotional album that defined the year. Dove Ellis continues that tradition with this chamber-pop / folk tour-de-force.

Not much is known about Ellis himself. The 22-year-old Galway born- artist now based in Manchester has never given an interview. He also doesn’t seem very interested in introducing himself. When Ellis uploaded a few songs to Bandcamp in late 2024, a bidding war followed, and he eventually chose a small indie label instead of any of the big label competitors. Speaking of Geese: at their sold-out US tour last year, they brought Ellis along as the opener, where he played his first ‘bigger’ shows. In contrast, he has only played a handful of headline shows himself. Yet it’s exactly that mystique and distance that seem to form part of his appeal.

Despite his limited interest in being a public figure, the hype for this debut album was undeniably strong. With lead single ‘To The Sandals’ (a reworked version of one of those earlier Bandcamp tracks), Ellis immediately showed that he has ‘it’. His voice brings hints of Jeff Buckley, and his approach to songwriting feels as deliberate as someone like a Cameron Winter, who I mentioned earlier.

Across the album’s ten songs, Ellis pulls us completely into his own melancholic little world. Although he clearly wears his influences on his sleeve, he still crafts a universe that is unmistakably his own. Take opener ‘Little Left Hope’, one of the more intimate moments on the record. The track initially evokes a hint of Nick Drake, slowly builds up, and eventually collapses back into silence. Something that happens more than just this one time on the album. ‘Jaundice’ is the shortest track here and presents a complete shift, with lyrics that read like an old poem and instrumentation that makes you want to dance as if you’re at a medieval festival.

The next two tracks, ‘Pale Song’ and ‘Love Is’, were released a few weeks ago as a double single and complement each other perfectly. ‘Pale Song’ continues the fragile atmosphere of the previous track, carried by a soft guitar and a few electronic *refinements*. At first glance, ‘Love Is’ seems to follow the same direction, but its placement feels almost deliberate to break the pattern and reveal Ellis’s range. The song quickly blooms into a warmer and livelier whole.

On ‘When You Tie Your Hair Up’, Ellis sounds completely defeated and hopeless. This track practically sums up the entire album with its almost otherworldly buildup and vocals. The way his voice moves from whisper-soft to desperately breaking open, while the instrumentation shifts from a single stringed instrument to a broader, airy texture, makes this the emotional center of the record. It feels as if he lays his entire inner world out on the table in that moment.

Throughout the rest of the album, Ellis continues to balance vulnerability and controlled intensity. Songs like ‘Heaven Has No Wings’ and ‘It Is A Blizzard’ show how effortlessly he shifts between intimate chamber-folk and the more layered arrangements. At no point does it feel like he overreaches; every instrument, every tiny sound, every vocal choice seems placed with precision to serve the whole. This makes Blizzard such a worthwhile listen, it’s a really coherent body of work that sinks deeper with every listen. (Which I’ve done my fair share of over the past few days)

What is absolutely clear on Blizzard is that the Manchester-based artist is one to watch. He has found his own sound without losing his spontaneity, and this debut genuinely feels like the start of something much bigger. You hear someone who knows exactly where he wants to go, yet still leaves room to surprise. If Ellis continues along this path, he could very well become another artist who shapes the rest of this decade.

John Lennon / Plastic Ono Band (1970)
John Lennon / Plastic Ono Band
Review by K_DOGG
What makes Plastic Ono Band so special? Some would say the songwriting, others would argue the "raw" production, but to me what makes this album such an interesting listen is John Lennon himself. For all his brokenness and cynicism, he did not shy away from who he was and how he saw the world, but rather spelled it out. The "art" of the project if you will, does not feel like a "creation" of John Lennon, but rather, this album is John Lennon, or at least as close as we will get to knowing who he was.

Lennon here feels lost. The start of the record begins with "Mother", which is one of the most emotionally powerful songs Lennon has ever performed, as he sings about his place in the world, with a father that left him and a mother which was gone too soon, and at the end begins to scream in longing for the relationships he never had. His questions become even stronger on "Working Class Hero", where John begins to simply wonder why society is the way that it is.

For as stray as Lennon feels on this album, he is just as equally angry. Listening to songs like "I Found Out" and "Remember", it's clear in many ways that he may not be completely lost, but actually very aware of the world he's in. Simply furious at it, and in many ways, rightfully so.

Despite much of the album's focus being on Lennon's catharsis, there are a few tracks that show a more idealistic side of John. One of acceptance. "Love" is perhaps the most idealized message here, and despite being very abstract, shows what I think Lennon truly idolized, and strove to achieve, even if he was a figure who had plenty of crucial missteps along the way. Ultimately, his biggest moment of clarity on the album is "God". Like a confession he shares his belief, as he strikes gods off his list, he triumphantly stops at "I don't believe in Beatles...", and after a pause he quietly whispers, "I just believe in me". Call it arrogant, but at the end of the day, it's honest. Whether or not you agree with this philosophy (I certainly don't), you can't call his bluff.

While this is one of the most personal recordings ever released, I don't think it's fair to call it unrelatable. I think there's a little bit of Lennon in all of us. While circumstances vary from person to person, one thing which is universal is one's humanity. And that's something that can't be created, but rather, captured. Plastic Ono Band is one of the most successful attempts at capturing one's humanity, and it won't be forgotten anytime soon.

10/10

Trisagion (2021)
Ethereal Shroud
Review by Memor
Have you ever come across an artist or album that feels like it's tailor-made just for you? Where the music affects you on a deeply personal level, even if the artist and their circumstances are worlds removed from your own? In January of 2016, I found my "soulmate". Some long-forgotten message board dweller, whose tastes aligned quite nicely with my own, mentioned the debut album They Became the Falling Ash by atmospheric black metal solo project Ethereal Shroud among his 2015 favourites, and the way they described the album spoke to me, so I gave it a listen, and it was instant love from the first "meeting".

The funeral doom-laden wintry atmosphere of They Became the Falling Ash caught me off guard and got really near to me, and not only did it end up as my 2015 album of the year as well, it has become one of my all-time favourites, regardless of genre. With just this album (and the 2013 demo that preceded it), Joseph Hawker entranced me completely, and I would have been completely content if nothing more was heard of Ethereal Shroud afterwards, even if I did keep hoping for the next chapter. That next chapter was set in motion in 2020, when Hawker released a remastered version of the debut, with promises of new original material just beyond the horizon. A short while later, Lanterns was released alongside information that this new song was one of four Hawker had been working on meticulously, and that a new full-length album, Trisagion, would be released in 2021, with Lanterns being chosen as the "preview" specifically because it would not appear on the album proper, due to not fitting in with its style.

Lanterns got me incredibly hyped for Trisagion, but when the fated date finally came and I could immerse myself in a new Ethereal Shroud experience, it turned out I was not at all prepared for what was to come. I was sure Trisagion would be fantastic, and probably land among my favourites for the year, but I didn't foresee it being this powerful. Trisagion, standing for "thrice holy", is an allusion to orthodox tradition, but don't take that to mean this is a religious album by any means. It does however work as one of the lyrical fountains of inspiration for Hawker, and the three songs here all adhere to the structure of the trisagion hymn, being in turn comprised of three movements.

But even if Trisagion isn't a religious work, it sure is a spiritual one. The album's themes are born from Hawker's cynicism toward the world, society and humanity overall, but even if those feelings mostly come from a place of anger, sorrow and desolation, Trisagion overturns that darkness into brilliant beauty on many occasions, and the overall effect of the album is surprisingly uplifting. They Became the Falling Ash already showed that Hawker's strongest suit, along with the powerful atmospheres he weaves, is his capability of creating beautiful, evocative and especially memorable lead melodies, and Trisagion doesn't only match his earlier ability, but outdoes it at every turn.

Stylistically Trisagion doesn't draw quite as heavily from funeral doom, but the elements are there in the background, especially in the less busy parts of Chasmal Fires and Astral Mariner. In turn, there's a certain symphonic quality to the sound that really elevates it to the next level. No, it's not symphonic black metal per se, but it definitely draws influence. The faster and more aggressive Discarnate dips into melodic black metal territory, but everything falls together very nicely, and the stylistical variations on the album never feel out of place or overdone. But most importantly, even if Trisagion brings a complete facelift to the "Ethereal Shroud sound", the atmosphere and sense for melody is so recognisable that it's in complete continuity with They Became the Falling Ash.

Compared to the "minimalism" of They Became the Falling Ash, Trisagion is a grandiose affair that pulls out all the stops. The strings are a new addition and complement both the mournful and uplifting moments perfectly! The more prominent synths find themselves in perfect balance with the surrounding music, always audible but never completely stealing the spotlight. The rumbly bass adds a nice touch in the quieter moments as well. The drums are highly energetic and intricate as well, really sealing the deal here. Not that the DIY aspect of They Became the Falling Ash takes anything away from its quality, but Hawker definitely made the right choice in reaching out to other musicans this time. Richard Spencer on bass and viola, John Kerr on drums and Shannon Greaves on vocals (Chasmal Fires), not to forget Spenser Morris' masterful work with mixing and mastering; while Trisagion is Hawker's vision and, practically, life's work, it was brought to life as a group effort, and the impact of the "guests" can not be understated. While the material here is, in some form, as old as They Became the Falling Ash, Hawker's decision to wait until he was confident in his ability to make Trisagion a reality definitely paid off!

I'll be the first to admit that They Became the Falling Ash's approach to that wintry, downhearted atmosphere was pretty melodramatic, in a "lie in the snow until you die" kind of way. Trisagion is certainly melancholic and extremely sentimental, but there's absolutely zero cheese here. Everything comes off as sincere and passionate, even the soft guest vocals by Greaves or the Saor -esque strings on Chasmal Fires. I wouldn't have thought I'd ever use the word "epic" to describe Ethereal Shroud, but Trisagion goes there, all the while sounding exactly like Ethereal Shroud feels like, if that makes sense. Like They Became the Falling Ash, Trisagion is so entrancing that the 64 minutes just positively fly by; Chasmal Fires is definitely the "shortest" 28-minute song I've ever heard.

Morris' production is the ultimate finishing touch on an already wonderful album; Trisagion is drenched in reverb without going so far that the reverb hides crucial details (or god forbid, to mask blemishes), and every element comes through clearly without overpowering anything else. Hawker's visceral screams pierce right through your soul, and the immaculate lead guitars are nigh otherworldly in quality. I'm not usually one to notice technical details, production-, instrumentation- or performance-wise, but man, that breath Hawker draws before the first opening scream in Chasmal Fires shakes me down to my core, and Morris' production draws your attention to it beautifully.

Trisagion honestly has me at a loss for words. It's deeply impactful and I love every second of it. I'm not one to tear up at music (or any other media for that matter), but during that first listen of Trisagion, I was constantly choked up. It's an utterly beautiful work that leaves me both devastated and optimistic; such is the curious balance of light and dark that Hawker achieves here. It's pure catharsis for me! In April of this year I remarked how I wouldn't be surprised if an album stronger than Über den Sternen by Empyrium would come out in 2021, but that there probably wouldn't be another to touch me as deeply. Trisagion, an album fifteen years in the making, is both of those things. Here it is, folks: my album of the year 2021!


Addendum: I don't usually talk about bonus tracks in my reviews, but Lanterns is an exception worth making. Hawker re-recorded the song after its original release in 2020, and it too was mixed and mastered by Morris to fit into the soundscape of the album, and it clears the final hurdle the song had in its original form. It's a beautiful, haunting piece of atmospheric black metal with a surprising, life-affirming blackgaze motif, and just as good as the songs on the album proper. I can definitely get behind Hawker's vision of it not fitting in within the context of the album, as Trisagion is perfect as it is, and Astral Mariner is the perfect closer, but should you choose to acquire a physical version of Trisagion in the future, be ready for a bonus track that stands up as equals with Hawker's best work.
